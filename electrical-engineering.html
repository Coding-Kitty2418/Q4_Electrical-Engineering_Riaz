According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, "Electrical engineers design, develop, test and supervise the manufacturing of electrical equipment, such as electric motors, radar and navigation systems, communications systems and power generation equipment."

If it's a real-world gadget that generates, conducts, or utilizes electricity, it was most likely built by an electrical engineer.

Electrical engineers of today create electrical devices and systems by combining fundamental components such as wires, coils, magnets, batteries, switches, resistors, capacitors, inductors, diodes, and transistors. These fundamental components are used in nearly all electrical and electronic devices, from power plant generators to microprocessors in your phone.





Skills required for every Electrical Engineer



Problem solving

In electrical engineering, you are often required to think logically and apply a particular rule or concept to a problem in order to solve it.

Organization

The ability to organize and manage your time is important for an electrical engineer, as much of your work will likely be time-sensitive or project-based, regardless which area of engineering you specialize in.

It's not just in the workplace that these skills will come in handy, though. Being able to manage it all and stay on top — especially during exam season — will stand you in good stead.

Critical thinking

Possessing the ability to approach things differently or take a different view to the norm can make a big difference when you’re trying to achieve a certain goal with your project.
This includes analyzing the strengths and weaknesses of the project or the problem in front of you, and offering alternative solutions, approaches and conclusions — all key aspects of critical thinking.

In-depth Mathematical Knowledge

Electrical engineering students often struggle because they have a weak mathematical foundation. So if you want to take electrical engineering as a career, focus on calculus at school and take as many maths-related electives as possible.

Basic circuit knowledge

Electrical engineering is an extremely challenging field, especially when dealing with massive installations (such as energy grids) or even highly modern pieces of tiny hardware (e.g. the ones used in smartphones). As a result, if you wish to comprehend it all, you must first have extensive knowledge of basic circuit design.

Creative thinking

Engineers are not just problem solvers, but also trailblazers. This is particularly true in the commercial sector, where electronics titans are continuously striving to produce new and innovative technologies; it is also a crucial trait that top engineering schools look for in potential applicants.

Remember: you might have all the information in the world, but if you don't know how to be inventive and explore fresh opportunities with it, you'll be left behind.

Communication

Communication skills are a crucial component of the profession, whether it's understanding a client's goals and objectives, working in teams on a project to build or enhance a piece of hardware, or interacting with other departments and stakeholders.

Programming

Although coding is more essential in some fields of electrical engineering than others, it is still a highly beneficial aptitude to have, especially when dealing with low-level embedded devices or analyzing data.

Electronic troubleshooting

Electronic troubleshooting is the identification, location, and correction of errors using measurement and diagnostic equipment. The amount of complexity varies based on the equipment you need to work with and repair; in certain circumstances, you may need to test each gadget component separately to ensure that everything functions correctly.

Knowing how to analyze, approach, and repair electrical circuits is the bread and butter of this trade, thus this is a talent that must be honed in order to advance in your career.

Leadership

As you develop in your career and pursue executive opportunities in your area, having solid project management abilities and the capacity to supervise a team, if not an entire organization, may be required.
